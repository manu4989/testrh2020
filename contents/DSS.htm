Speed vs Power

The Speed vs. Power curve is one way to understand how the ship is performing and its fuel consumption over time.

Background info

The Speed vs Power curve should be prepared during the delivery sea trials and is the base line by which to compare the curves throughout the ship’s life. At the beginning of a ship’s life, Power is a more stable parameter than fuel consumption and hence easier to measure. The corresponding ship speed is measured and is the most significant parameter in determining both the power and the fuel consumption.

The curve is based on three main parameters:

draft and displacement

meteorological conditions (wind and sea)

hull and propeller roughness

It’s normal for the Speed vs Power curve to worsen over time, as the condition of the hull and propeller deteriorates.

Trim vs Wasted Power

Trim is the difference between the draft forward and the draft aft of a ship. The trim has a significant effect on the vessel’s resistance/propulsive power; it’s important to try to maintain the optimum trim every voyage to save energy and reduce emissions. For this reason, the curve that corresponds to the Trim Over Time is reported in the same graph as the Optimum Trim and the Wasted Power (calculated considering the Effective Trim).

The horizontal axis represents velocity; the vertical axis represents the percentage of the times the ship reached that velocity in the selected time range. This analysis lets you see how the velocity of the ship fluctuated over a period of time.

Main Engine

This section of Performance analyzes the ship’s Diesel Generator(s). The main purpose of this section is to:

let you to compare how the Diesel Generator(s) is performing in a specific moment of ship’s life, to the performance registered during the ship’s sea trials

define machinery trends to help you pre-empt breakdowns and make maintenance decisions

calculate the fuel consumption of the ship

(For more information on tools and tips for using this section of Analytics, see Functions for Navigating Analytics.)

Daily Consumption

In this calculation, Performance takes the following information into consideration:

Speed of the vessel (when speed increases by 30%, fuel consumption can double)

Displacement

Weather (weather can affect the fuel consumption by up to 6%)

Phase of the voyage (when the ship is anchored in port, fuel consumption is about 15% of the fuel consumed at sea)

Time (time calculated by day)

The Daily Consumption is shown in comparison with the Speed Over Ground

Main Engine SFOC (Specific Fuel Oil Consumption)

Main Engine SFOC unit of measure is:

g/(kW x Hr)

g = Grams à weight

kW = kiloWatt à power

Hr = Hour à time

This means that the SFOC represents how much fuel is consumed considering the power applied by the Main Engine during a specific period.

The SFOC curve has a direct connection with NOx emissions (if SFOC is reduced, NOx emissions generally increase), so this is an important index considering all the rules & regulations that require NOx emissions to be cut by as much as possible. The target of each ship should be optimum SFOC.

When a single ship is selected, the dots represent the status of the Propulsion Efficiency compared to the target value at that point in time.

Engine RPM—Power

This analysis is based on the Engine performance curves that are given by the Maker.

The available curves are shown on the graph, the default are:

Engine Limits (yellow): represents the threshold between normal engine load and overload

B.P. Curve Heavy Propeller Curve (violet): the propeller power request when the effort to push the ship is higher than design condition

Nominal Propeller Curve (green): the propeller power request at nominal design conditions

Light Running Propeller Curve (light blue): the propeller power request when pushing a lighter vessel

Engine Overload (red): the upper limit of engine when working in overload conditions

Dots (pink): show the actual workload of the engine in the selected period

Ideally, the engine should be working at a load that keeps it between the pink (light load) and the light green (nominal load) curves.

Engine RPM%—MCR

This graph shows the same information as Engine RPM—Power, the difference being that here the MCR (Maximum Continuous Rating) is expressed as a % instead of an integer.

Speed vs Cons per Hour

The relation between Speed and Fuel Consumption is not linear but exponential. The result of Consumption per Hour is that when speed increases, consumption increases as well.

Consumption per Mile/Hour

This graph compares the Consumption per Mile with the Target SFOC and Main Engine Consumption per Hour. Consumption per Hour is the most accurate representation of the actual consumption of the engine(s).

These are two different ways of looking at how much an engine is consuming over time.

The DG Consumption can be calculated different ways depending on what is available on board:

using flowmeters

based on total generated power in combination with the SFOC target

SFOC is a calculation that tells you how much fuel the engine uses to generate 1 kW of power.

Power Generation—DG Running vs No. Reefers (Refrigerated Containers)

This analysis is for ships that use reefers to transport commodities that require temperature-controlled transportation. A ship may have the refrigeration system turned down, off, or up, depending on the phase of the voyage. This information is valuable because it helps you detect when you have a higher number of diesel generators running and what the reason for them running may be. If there are many reefers on board it makes sense that more power is requested. If there aren’t many reefers working and you are still consuming lots of power, you know you need to look into why.

In the graph, you can see two phases during the period selected:

first phase: the refrigeration system is running about 80 reefers and the ship is transporting some cargo

second phase: there are around 180 reefers running, so probably more cargo has been loaded on the vessel and that cargo needs to be refrigerated

Power Generation—DG Power vs No. Reefers

There is a correlation between a ship’s DG Power expressed and the number of reefers in use, because the DG(s) generate power for part of the refrigeration system.

The graph shows that when the number of reefers in use decreases, the DG Power applied decreases, too.

ISO 19030 Analysis—Intro (Full Installation only)

Analyze hull and propulsion performance to make sure ships are in line with ISO 19030.

A really brief summary of the ISO 19030 is:

“The aim of the standard is to prescribe practical methods for measuring changes in ship specific hull and propeller performance and to define a set of relevant performance indicators for hull and propeller maintenance, repair and retrofit activities. The methods are not intended for comparing the performance of ships of different types and sizes (including sister ships) nor to be used in a regulatory framework.”

Dry-Dock Performance

Hull and propeller performance directly following dry-dock maintenance is compared to their performance following the last dry-dock. This provides useful information on the effectiveness of the dry-dock.

In-Service

In-service performance is the average change in hull and propeller performance over the dry-docking interval. Performance over the first year following the dry-dock is compared with performance over whatever remains of the docking interval—typically two to four years. This performance indicator is useful for determining the effectiveness of the underwater hull and propeller solution (for example the hull coating used).

Maintenance Trigger

The indicator of hull and propeller performance at the start of the dry-docking interval is compared with a moving average at a point in time. Determine when hull and propeller maintenance—including propeller polishing or hull cleaning—needs to be done as defined in the standard.

Maintenance Effect

Hull and propeller performance in the period preceding maintenance is compared with their performance after, which helps determine how effective maintenance is.
